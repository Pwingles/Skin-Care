Steps 01 — 06

The Surface
Routine

Six precisely ordered steps — morning and evening. Each one validated by clinical evidence. Together, they form the unbreakable foundation of every high-performing skincare protocol.

01
Foundation

Cleansing

Night cleansing is non-negotiable. Morning is optional. Most people do this backwards — washing twice daily strips the skin's protective barrier, leading to 40–60% faster moisture loss and slower recovery.

The rule: if you worked out or woke up genuinely oily, cleanse in the morning. Otherwise, skip it and protect what your skin built overnight.

  • Look for ceramides, fatty acids, or cholesterol
  • Avoid sulfates, fragrances, and bar soaps
  • Oily or acne-prone → foaming cleanser
  • Dry or sensitive → non-foaming hydrating cleanser
The Science

Harsh cleansers raise your skin's pH above its optimal 4.5–5.5 range. This disrupts the acid mantle, collapses lipid bilayers, and studies confirm it causes 40–60% faster transepidermal water loss — directly sabotaging everything else you apply.

02
Morning Protection

Vitamin C Serum

Applied after cleansing, before moisturizer. This is your daily armor against oxidative damage. Every day, UV rays, pollution, and stress attack your skin — breaking down collagen, triggering free radical cascades, and accelerating aging. Vitamin C neutralizes that damage before it accumulates.

Even better: it actively stimulates new collagen production, smoothing fine lines over time — not just preventing new ones.

  • Use L-ascorbic acid — the most researched, most effective form
  • 15–20% concentration is the clinical sweet spot
  • Must be combined with Vitamin E + ferulic acid for 8× effectiveness and stability
  • If it turns brown, it's oxidized — replace it
  • Store refrigerated or in a dark glass bottle
The Science

Studies show combining L-ascorbic acid with Vitamin E and ferulic acid increases antioxidant effectiveness by up to 8× and prevents oxidation — the primary failure mode of most Vitamin C serums on the market.

03
Targeted Treatment

Azelaic Acid (conditional)

Only if you're dealing with dark spots, post-acne marks, melasma, or redness. Skip this step if you have none of these concerns — you can always add it later. Applied in the morning after Vitamin C, before moisturizer.

Azelaic acid works on two levels: it blocks melanin production to fade pigmentation, and reduces inflammatory pathways that cause redness and rosacea.

  • 10% — over-the-counter, for mild pigmentation and redness
  • 15–20% — prescription strength for stubborn melasma or rosacea
  • Unlike retinoids, does not increase UV sensitivity
  • Pairs synergistically with tretinoin for superior results
  • Well-tolerated even on sensitive skin
The Science

A 24-week study comparing 20% azelaic acid to 0.05% tretinoin for melasma found both equally effective at reducing pigmentation — but azelaic acid caused significantly less irritation, peeling, and redness, making it the safer first choice.

Skip this step if you have no pigmentation, dark spots, or redness concerns. It's not needed in a pure anti-aging or maintenance routine.

04
Repair

Niacinamide

Vitamin B3 — one of the most studied and versatile ingredients in skincare. It fades dark spots, evens skin tone, reduces sebum production, and measurably strengthens your barrier so your skin holds onto moisture better over time.

It works on multiple pathways simultaneously, which is why it plays well with almost every other active in this protocol.

  • 2–5% — optimal for most skin types
  • Up to 10% — for oily skin or visibly enlarged pores
  • Above 10% causes irritation with zero additional benefit
  • Compatible with retinoids, Vitamin C, and azelaic acid
  • Can be built into your moisturizer (see CeraVe PM) to simplify the routine
The Science

Multiple peer-reviewed studies confirm 2–5% niacinamide reduces hyperpigmentation, sebum output, and pore appearance while measurably improving epidermal barrier function — making it one of the highest evidence-to-cost-ratio ingredients available.

05
Seal & Protect

Moisturizer

After cleansing and applying actives, your skin is more vulnerable to moisture loss — up to 40–60% faster without a proper occlusive layer. Moisturizer seals every previous step in and rebuilds the lipid barrier your skin depends on.

The biggest mistake people make: choosing moisturizers with fragrances or alcohol. These actively damage the barrier you're trying to protect. The formula matters as much as the routine.

  • Dry skin → rich ceramide cream with cholesterol and hyaluronic acid
  • Oily skin → lightweight non-comedogenic lotion
  • Apply while skin is slightly damp for optimal absorption
  • No fragrances, no alcohol, no unnecessary additives

06
Sun Defense

Sunscreen

Dermatology research estimates 80% of visible aging is caused by UV exposure — not genetics, not diet. But you don't need sunscreen every single day, and most advice gets this wrong.

The rule: If you're going outside between 10am–4pm, wear it. Indoors all day? Skip it. Early morning sunlight (first 30–60 minutes after waking) is actually beneficial — UV levels are too low to damage skin but sufficient for circadian rhythm anchoring and vitamin D.

  • Always choose mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide)
  • Chemical filters (avobenzone, oxybenzone) have been detected in blood and breast milk after regular use
  • Mineral formulas sit on top of skin and physically block UV — they don't cross into the bloodstream
  • If you shower mid-day, re-apply your full morning routine
  • No sunscreen needed for pre-10am outdoor activity
The Science

Clinical trials have detected chemical sunscreen ingredients (avobenzone, oxybenzone, octocrylene) in blood plasma, urine, and breast milk after standard topical use — exceeding FDA thresholds requiring further safety data. Mineral alternatives have not shown this systemic absorption profile.

Evening Protocol

The Night Routine

Nighttime is when your skin actually repairs itself. The evening protocol introduces the single most important anti-aging ingredient in existence — retinoids. But the sequence and frequency matter as much as the ingredient itself.

Why Retinoids Are Different

Most skincare products only reach the epidermis — the outer layer. Retinoids penetrate into the dermis, where collagen is made and aging actually happens. A 12-week study found 0.3% retinol reduced fine lines by 44% and improved skin texture in 80% of participants. A tretinoin study showed 68% improvement in photoaging after 48 weeks. No other topical comes close.

Retinol Nights — Mon / Wed / Fri

Active Night

  • Gentle cleanse
  • Hyaluronic acid serum (optional, for hydration)
  • Retinol (0.01–0.1% to start, build slowly)
  • Azelaic acid (if pigmentation concern)
  • CeraVe PM moisturizer (niacinamide + ceramides)
Recovery Nights — Tue / Thu / Sat / Sun

Recovery Night

  • Gentle cleanse
  • Hyaluronic acid serum
  • Niacinamide serum or CeraVe PM
  • Rich moisturizer (let skin rebuild)
  • No actives — barrier recovery only
