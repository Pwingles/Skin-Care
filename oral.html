Steps 01 — 05

The Oral
Routine

Five precisely ordered steps — built on clinical evidence, not dental marketing. The sequence matters. The technique matters. Together, they form the foundation of a mouth that protects your whole body, not just your teeth.

01
First Step

Flossing

Floss before brushing — not after. A 2018 clinical study directly compared the two sequences and found the floss-first group showed significantly greater plaque reduction than the brush-first group. The mechanism is simple: flossing first dislodges interdental plaque and debris, then brushing sweeps it all away.

When it comes to floss type, dental tape outperforms both wax floss and super floss for interproximal plaque removal. But technique matters far more than the type you choose — a perfectly executed string floss routine beats lazy water flossing every time.

  • Wrap floss around middle fingers, control with thumb and pointer
  • Use a gentle saw motion to guide it between teeth
  • Hug each tooth in a C-shape and slide under the gum line
  • Up-and-down motion on both surfaces of every gap
  • Never snap floss aggressively — it cuts gum tissue
The Science

Dental tape showed the greatest improvement in interproximal plaque scores in a comparative study of floss types, while super floss performed lowest. However, consistency and correct technique remain the dominant factors — the best floss is the one you'll actually use correctly every day.

Mazhari et al., J Periodontol 2018

02
Before You Brush

Tongue Scraping

The tongue harbors one of the densest bacterial communities in the entire body. Overnight, these bacteria metabolize amino acids and produce volatile sulfur compounds — the primary cause of bad breath and a direct driver of gum line inflammation. One clinical trial found tongue scraping reduces volatile sulfur compounds by 75%, while toothbrush-based tongue cleaning only achieved 45%.

Do this in the morning, before breakfast. Bacterial metabolism peaks overnight — by scraping before eating, you prevent swallowing metabolites that trigger systemic inflammation. Always scrape before brushing so you're not spreading tongue bacteria across clean teeth.

  • Stick your tongue out as far as possible
  • Place the scraper as far back as you can comfortably reach
  • Gently press down and slowly drag forward to the tip
  • Key word: gently — this is not aggressive scrubbing
  • Rinse and repeat 2–3 times
The Science

A crossover study comparing metal scrapers, plastic scrapers, and toothbrushes found both metal and plastic scrapers showed equivalent bacterial count reduction, both outperforming toothbrush-based cleaning. Volatile sulfur compound reduction with scraping: 75% vs 45% for brushing alone.

03
Core Step

Brushing

Multiple studies confirm oscillating-rotating electric toothbrushes produce a 20% greater reduction in plaque and gingivitis compared to manual brushing. Within electric types, a 12-week clinical trial found oscillating-rotating brushes outperformed sonic brushes on both measures. The oscillating-rotating mechanism physically disrupts plaque — always choose this over sonic.

The most overlooked rule: 7.5 seconds per tooth surface. Most people rush and under-brush their inner teeth. The inner surfaces statistically receive the least time — deliberately applying the 7.5-second rule there will make the biggest difference. Studies also show brushing more than twice daily significantly increases enamel loss.

  • Brush maximum twice daily — over-brushing erodes enamel permanently
  • Apply gentle, not aggressive pressure — gum recession doesn't reverse
  • 7.5 seconds per surface, inner teeth especially
  • Change brush head every 3 months — worn bristles cut effectiveness significantly
  • Let the oscillating head do the work; your job is positioning and gentle contact
The Science

Excessive brushing pressure causes mechanical abrasion of the gingiva — the gum tissue. Over time, this leads to gum recession. Once gum tissue recedes, it does not grow back naturally. The Oral-B iO Series 9 features a pressure sensor that alerts you when you're pressing too hard — one of the most clinically useful features in any consumer dental device.

Yaacob et al., Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2014

Toothpaste

The fluoride vs. hydroxyapatite debate is nuanced. Over 70 years of fluoride research shows clear cavity reduction — but cumulative ingestion over years from twice-daily brushing becomes a meaningful exposure. Nano-hydroxyapatite, the same mineral your enamel is made of, has been shown in a 2022 RCT to perform similarly to 1450 ppm fluoride toothpaste for preventing early cavities, with a completely different and entirely safe mechanism.

How Hydroxyapatite Works

Instead of forming a protective fluorapatite layer, nano-hydroxyapatite acts as a direct building block — binding to micro-cracks in enamel and reinforcing them. It remineralizes without any systemic absorption concerns.

RCT: nano-HA vs 1450 ppm fluoride, 2022

04
Sub-Gum Cleaning

Water Flosser

Brushing alone misses nearly 40% of your plaque biofilm — specifically the subgingival bacteria living beneath the gum line where bristles can't physically reach without damaging tissue. The water flosser disrupts this biofilm through pulsed hydraulic pressure, reaching areas conventional floss cannot.

A clinical study found that adding a water flosser to a brush + floss routine produced a 51% greater reduction in gum bleeding and a 29% greater reduction in plaque index compared to brush + floss alone. Beyond oral health, a 2021 study of type 2 diabetes patients found daily water flosser use reduced systemic CRP levels after 12 weeks — inflammation markers measurable beyond the mouth.

Use after brushing. Though some researchers recommend before, using it after brushing clears toothpaste residue you'd otherwise swallow — a meaningful long-term consideration given fluoride accumulation data.

  • Start at the lowest pressure setting — high pressure causes gum bleeding in beginners
  • As gum inflammation reduces over weeks, bleeding stops and you can increase pressure
  • Use a dedicated gum-tip attachment, not just the standard jet tip
  • Add a tongue tip for comprehensive oral coverage
  • Aim the tip at a 90° angle to the gum line and move slowly tooth to tooth
The Science

Water flossers disrupt the bacterial matrix of biofilm below the gum line — a task conventional floss can't achieve without physically damaging tissue. The pulsation frequency creates hydraulic shear forces that penetrate 6mm beneath the gum margin in clinical measurements.

J Clin Dent — water flosser vs brush+floss

05
Final Step

Oil Pulling

Most mouthwashes use chlorhexidine — a broad-spectrum antibacterial that reduces oral bacteria by over 97% in 24 hours. But a 2016 study found chlorhexidine also kills the beneficial nitrate-reducing bacteria your body depends on for blood pressure regulation. A 2019 study in Free Radical Biology and Medicine found one week of chlorhexidine mouthwash use raised systolic blood pressure by 2–3 mmHg — a meaningful cardiovascular signal from a product marketed as routine dental hygiene.

Oil pulling works differently. It doesn't sterilize — it balances. A 2017 trial comparing coconut oil pulling to chlorhexidine found both reduced Streptococcus mutans (the primary cavity-causing bacteria) by equivalent amounts. The difference: oil pulling left beneficial nitrate-reducing bacteria untouched.

  • Swish 1 tablespoon of oil for 10–20 minutes
  • The oil emulsifies and traps bacteria through fat-binding
  • Spit into a bin — not the sink (oil solidifies in pipes)
  • Do not swallow — the oil contains trapped bacteria and toxins
  • Rinse with water afterward
The Science

Oil pulling works through mechanical emulsification — the fat molecules physically bind to and encapsulate bacteria as you swish, gradually clearing them from oral surfaces. Unlike antibacterial mouthwash, it doesn't disrupt the oral microbiome or enter systemic circulation.

Oil pulling vs chlorhexidine — Strep mutans, 2017

On timing: Oil pull in the morning before eating — bacterial load peaks overnight and the oil will capture the maximum bacterial concentration before food disrupts the oral environment.

Beyond the Routine

5 Rules That Make
Your Teeth Stronger

The routine cleans. These rules protect. Follow both and enamel stays strong for life.

01

Brush Maximum Twice Daily

Brushing more than twice daily significantly increases enamel loss. Your enamel is like a glass shield — once it erodes, it will never grow back. Twice is optimal; three or more is destructive.

02

Wait 30–60 Min After Eating

Food — especially acidic food and drink — temporarily softens your enamel. Brushing immediately is like sanding soft wood. A 2018 study found brushing within 10 minutes of an acidic drink caused up to 60% more enamel erosion versus waiting 30 minutes.

03

Protect Your Saliva Flow

Saliva is your mouth's natural defense system — it neutralizes acid, remineralizes enamel, and limits bacterial growth. Dehydration, caffeine, alcohol, and mouth breathing all reduce saliva flow. Stay hydrated and breathe through your nose.

04

Limit Sugar Intake

When bacteria digest sugar, they produce lactic acid. Once your mouth pH drops below 5.5, enamel begins to demineralize — it literally dissolves. The issue isn't sugar touching teeth; it's the acid cascade it triggers. Limit frequency, not just quantity.

05

Remineralize Actively

Enamel can't heal, but it can remineralize — and that entirely depends on your nutrient status. Calcium provides the building blocks. Vitamin D transports and deposits calcium. Magnesium keeps calcium in balance so it goes to teeth, not arteries.

Prevention

The Dental Checkup
Protocol

Getting checked up is one thing. Knowing what the numbers mean is another. These are the only five metrics that matter.

Annual Schedule

How Often to Go

  • Two professional cleanings every year
  • One full exam per year with X-rays, clinical measurements, and oral cancer screening
  • One bite and airway evaluation every 1–2 years
Why It Matters

The Bite & Airway Exam

  • Almost no one requests this — almost everyone needs it
  • Your bite affects breathing mechanics, jaw stability, and head posture
  • Poor bite alignment is a root cause of sleep-disordered breathing
  • Correcting alignment improves nasal airflow and nitric oxide production
Remineralization

Nutrients for Strong Teeth

  • Calcium — the structural building block of enamel and dentin
  • Vitamin D — required for calcium absorption and transport
  • Magnesium — keeps calcium depositing in teeth, not arteries
  • Low sugar + low acid diet to prevent enamel demineralization

The 5 Numbers to Track

Ask your dentist for these specific measurements at every exam.

01
Plaque Index

Measures how much plaque is adhered to tooth surfaces. Lower = cleaner, less bacterial load, less inflammation.

Target: 0
02
Gingival Index

Measures gum inflammation on a 0–4 scale by gum colour. Healthy gum is light pink and does not bleed when touched.

Target: 0
03
Pocket Depth

The most critical number. Measures space between tooth and gum. Once deep pockets form, bacteria live there permanently.

Target: < 3mm
04
Bone Levels

Measured from X-rays. Bone loss is irreversible. Compare images across visits — any downward trend needs immediate attention.

Stable
05
Bite Evaluation

Assesses occlusion and airway. Misalignment drives plaque retention, chewing inefficiency, jaw tension, and sleep-disordered breathing.

Every 1–2 yrs

Pocket depth warning: 4mm = early gum disease. 5mm or more means bacteria are living beneath your gum line 24 hours a day. This is reversible at 4mm with aggressive intervention, but at 5mm+ bone loss typically begins. Catch it at 3mm.
